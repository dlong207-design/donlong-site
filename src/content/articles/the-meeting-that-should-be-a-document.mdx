There's a meeting you've sat through dozens of times. Someone presents a status update. A few questions get asked. Nothing is decided. Everyone leaves to return to the work they were doing before the meeting started.

This meeting exists because the information being shared in it isn't available any other way. It exists because the organization hasn't built the information infrastructure that would make the meeting unnecessary.

That's the real problem. And the solution isn't to cancel the meeting—it's to fix the information flow that the meeting is patching.

<Divider />

## Meetings as Information Infrastructure

Meetings exist for two genuinely useful purposes: making decisions that require synchronous discussion, and building relationships that require human presence. Status updates require neither.

Status is information. Information has better delivery mechanisms than a synchronous meeting: shared documents, dashboards, async video summaries, written updates with a clear structure. All of these get information to the people who need it without requiring everyone to be available at the same time.

When status updates are delivered in meetings, it's usually because:
- No one has built an alternative system
- The culture equates "showing up" with "staying informed"
- Leaders want visibility and meetings feel like the safest way to get it

None of these are good reasons. And none of them improve when you add another meeting.

<Callout type="note">
  This is not an argument against all meetings. Meetings are the right tool for decisions that require real-time discussion and alignment. They are the wrong tool for status updates, reporting, and information sharing that can be consumed asynchronously.
</Callout>

## What Replaces the Meeting

Replacing a status meeting requires building a replacement information system. This is a small investment with large returns.

**The written update.** A structured document, published on a regular cadence, that answers: what was completed, what's in progress, what's blocked, and what's coming next. Written well, this takes 20 minutes to produce and five minutes to read. It replaces an hour of meeting time for everyone.

**The visible tracker.** A shared space—simple, maintained—where the status of key work is always current. Not a project management system that requires training to read. A simple, readable format that anyone can check in 60 seconds.

**The async check-in.** A short recorded video or voice memo that replaces the "quick sync" that tends to become a 30-minute meeting. Useful for nuance that doesn't transfer well in writing.

## What the Meeting Time Is For

When you remove status from meeting agendas, two things happen:

First, the meeting gets shorter or disappears entirely. This is the obvious outcome.

Second—and more valuable—you recover time for the discussions that actually require a meeting: the hard decisions, the escalations, the strategic questions that deserve focused attention and honest debate.

<PullQuote>
  The goal isn't fewer meetings. It's meetings with higher signal. When status updates move out of meeting agendas, the meetings that remain are more valuable.
</PullQuote>

## The Real Resistance

If this is so straightforward, why do status meetings persist?

Because the meeting serves other functions that nobody states explicitly. It creates a forcing function for accountability—if you have to report on it in a meeting, you have to have something to report. It creates a ritual that people find orienting. It gives leaders the feeling of control that visibility provides.

These needs are real. But they can be met more efficiently. The accountability forcing function works in written updates. The ritual can be preserved in a brief, high-signal weekly format. The leadership visibility can come from a well-maintained tracker.

The question is whether the organization is willing to invest in building the alternative system—or whether the familiar meeting is easier.

<Divider />

Most organizational meetings exist because organizations haven't built better alternatives. The fix is to build the alternative: a lightweight, consistent information system that gets the right information to the right people without requiring everyone to be in the same room at the same time.

The meeting that should be a document is not a scheduling problem. It's an information architecture problem. Solve the architecture.
