Most managers think about delegation as task assignment. Hand work to someone else, free up your time, ensure it gets done. This is not delegation—it's distribution. And the difference matters enormously.

Delegation, done properly, is about transferring judgment—the ability to make the same decisions you would make, without requiring you to be in the room. That's a much harder and more valuable thing to accomplish.

<Divider />

## Why Managers Struggle to Delegate

The two most common failure modes are opposite but related.

**Underdelegation:** The manager holds on to work they should pass down, either because they believe no one else can do it well enough, or because they haven't invested in developing the people who could. The result: the manager is a bottleneck, the team doesn't grow, and organizational capacity stays capped.

**Overdelegation:** The manager passes work without transferring the context, standards, or support that would make the work executable. The result: poor output, frustrated team members, and a manager who concludes that delegation "doesn't work."

Both failures share a common root: the manager is delegating outputs instead of judgment.

## The Context Problem

When you hand someone a task without context, you're asking them to reverse-engineer your thinking. They'll make assumptions you didn't intend. They'll optimize for the wrong thing. And when the output doesn't meet your expectations, the failure will feel like a competence problem when it was actually a communication problem.

Effective delegation starts with transferring context:

- What is the goal behind the task?
- What does success look like, specifically?
- What tradeoffs should be made if constraints arise?
- What does the person have authority to decide, and what requires escalation?

<PullQuote>
  You cannot delegate execution without delegating understanding. The manager who holds the context will always be the bottleneck.
</PullQuote>

## The Standards Problem

Context tells people what to do. Standards tell them how well.

Most managers haven't articulated their standards explicitly—they hold them implicitly and apply them in review. This means the person doing the work doesn't know what standard they're trying to meet until they're told the output falls short.

This is a solvable problem. Before delegating any significant piece of work, spend five minutes articulating the standard:

- What does "good enough" look like?
- What would make this excellent?
- What are the non-negotiables?

These answers don't have to be long. They have to be explicit.

<Callout type="insight">
  The single most effective thing a manager can do to improve delegation outcomes is to clarify standards before work starts, not after it's submitted. This shifts feedback from reactive to proactive—and dramatically reduces rework.
</Callout>

## Building Toward Real Delegation

Delegation at its most useful is developmental. The goal isn't just to get this task done—it's to build someone's capacity to handle this category of work without you.

That means:
- Starting with lower-stakes work to build the pattern
- Giving feedback that explains reasoning, not just outcome
- Expanding scope as judgment is demonstrated
- Making the implicit explicit: "Here's how I'm thinking about this"

Real delegation creates leverage. The manager who has delegated well has created organizational capacity that didn't exist before. The team can handle more, with less, and the manager can focus where they actually add the most value.

<Divider />

Delegation isn't about freeing up calendar time. It's about building organizational capacity. The managers who do it well don't just distribute work—they transfer judgment. And that investment pays dividends over months and years, not days.
